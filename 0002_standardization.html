  1. To compare mortality and morbidity rates in two or more populations.This is important for the evaluation of community health status.
  2. Types of standardization:
    1. direct.
    2. indirect.
  3. Availability of age-standardized rates shall determine which standardization method to use.
    • If age-specific mortality rates (asmr) for two or more populations are known, use direct standardization.
    • If asmr are unknown, use indirect standardization.
  4. Direct standardization steps:
    1. Calculate asmr (each age group, each population).
    2. Choose one of the populations as reference (standard) population. (*If the mortality rates of a specific community are compared to the national population, then the national population is considered as reference).
    3. Multiply asmr of population under study to the number of persons in each age group of the standard population.
    4. We'll get the expected deaths for each age group, each population.
    5. Add the number of expected deaths from all age groups.
    6. To get age-adjusted mortality rates, divide total number of expected deaths by the standard population.
    7. Compare asmr of the two populations.
  5. Direct standardization, example:
    • B have higher crude death rates (cdr) than A.
    • Next, use national population as reference.
    • Expected death:
      • Population A : 1186.18
      • Population B : 1158.99
    • Age adjusted death rate:
      • A = (1186.18/51,000) x 1000 = 23.3 per 1000 population.
      • B = (1158.99/51,000) x 1000 = 22.7 per 1000 population.
    • Based on age-adjusted rates, risk of death is higher in population A than B.
    • Therefore, relying on crude death rates only is misleading.
  6. Indirect standardization steps:
    1. Choose a reference population.
    2. Calculate observed deaths in the populations of interest.
    3. Apply asmr from reference population to the populations of interest.
    4. Multiply people in each age group of the populations, by the asmr in the reference population.
    5. Sum the total expected deaths for each population.
    6. Divide the total observed deaths, by expected deaths.
    7. The ratio of observed deaths to expected deaths is “Standardized mortality ratio” (smr).
    8. smr = od/ed
    9. Calculate adjusted mortality rates (amr):
      • amr = smr x cdr
    10. If asmr of the reference population is applied, cdr must be calculated from that reference population.
  7. Indirect standardization, example:
    • First, we must know the observed death:
      • A = 120
      • B = 30
    • Tabulate data.
    • Calculate standardized mortality ratio (smr):
      • smr A = 120/195.5 = 0.61
      • smr B = 30/69.5 = 0.43
    • Still, risk of death is higher in population A than B, after adjusting for differences by age.
    • In indirect method, common practice is to compare smr.
